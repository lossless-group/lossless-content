# The Open Source Initiative

![Open Source Initiative (OSI) circular keyhole logo on a white background](https://opensource.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/008-open-source.svg)

_The Open Source Initiative is a globally influential nonprofit stewarding the definition, licensing norms, and policy agenda for open source software in the digital economy._[4][5]

The organization is a **nonprofit standards and advocacy body** that defines what “open source” means in licensing terms and acts as a neutral steward of open source licensing and community governance.[4][5] It maintains and interprets the Open Source Definition, approves licenses as compliant, and convenes a global community around open source policy, security, and sustainability.[4][1] Founded in the late 1990s and operating with a global footprint rather than a single-country focus, it is a central reference point for governments, enterprises, and developers evaluating “open source” claims.[5][3] Consultants track it because its guidance shapes software licensing strategy, compliance risk, and the evolving policy environment around open source and AI.[1][5]

## Mission and Identity

- **Stated mission (verbatim):** OSI describes its role as providing “neutral stewardship of open source licensing” and enabling “a global community,” emphasizing that “they trust in our neutral stewardship of open source licensing and our role in enabling a global community.”[4] It also characterizes itself as the body that maintains “the definition of ‘open source software,’” focusing on specific conditions licenses must satisfy.[5]

- OSI positions itself as the **neutral steward** of open source licensing, maintaining the Open Source Definition and vetting licenses against it to protect the integrity of the term “open source.”[4][5] It portrays itself as serving a global ecosystem of developers, maintainers, enterprises, and policymakers who “trust” its role in licensing and rely on it to navigate evolving issues like AI, security, and compliance.[4][1][5] Its self-image foregrounds neutrality, community governance, and the importance of open collaboration as a public good in digital infrastructure.[4][2]

- **Stated values / principles:** OSI emphasizes principles embodied in the Open Source Definition (such as free redistribution, source code availability, and nondiscrimination) and highlights “neutral stewardship,” “global community,” and support for “maintainers who keep critical digital infrastructure running” as core tenets.[4][2][5]

## What They Do

OSI’s day-to-day work centers on **maintaining the Open Source Definition, reviewing and approving open source licenses, and leading research and advocacy on open source software and policy.**[5][1] It generates impact through license stewardship, convening community discussions, publishing research like the State of Open Source report, and collaborating with platforms and organizations on initiatives such as Maintainer Month.[1][2][6]

Key activities and programs:

- **Maintain the Open Source Definition and license list** — OSI is cited as maintaining the definition of “open source software” via ten specific licensing conditions and as the reference for licenses that comply with this definition.[5][3]

- **License review and approval** — Acts as the neutral body whose approval signals that a software license complies with the Open Source Definition, thereby informing legal, business, and community adoption decisions.[5][3]

- **Policy and advocacy on open source and AI** — Engages with policymakers and industry on topics such as “open source AI,” with external analyses describing OSI as the authority on what qualifies as open source and referencing its criteria in AI policy debates.[5]

- **Research and reporting** — Produces the “State of Open Source” report, analyzing trends such as how open source has become a strategic concern for IT leadership due to “geopolitical pressure, security risk, compliance complexity, and the growing operational burden of maintaining open source software at scale.”[1]

- **Community and ecosystem convening** — Hosts and tracks open source events worldwide, including conferences intersecting with AI, cloud, and cybersecurity, and encourages community discussion via channels such as its discussion forums.[6][4]

- **Support and recognition for maintainers** — Participates in “Maintainer Month,” joining GitHub and the broader community to “recognize, support, and celebrate the maintainers who keep critical digital infrastructure running.”[2]

- **Education and outreach** — Through blogs, reports, and public commentary, it educates organizations and governments on open source licensing, security, and sustainability challenges.[1][4]

## History and Origin Story

OSI emerged in the late 1990s as a response to the need to clearly define “open source” and differentiate open source licensing from other forms of free or shared software, positioning itself as the steward of a formal Open Source Definition.[3][5] Over time it became the de facto authority for determining whether a license is “open source,” expanded into policy and research roles (including State of Open Source reports), and increased its engagement with issues like AI and critical digital infrastructure maintenance.[1][5][2]

Key inflection points:

- **Late 1990s** — OSI is established as a nonprofit body to maintain and promote the Open Source Definition and to approve compliant licenses, creating a central authority for open source licensing in parallel with the rise of free and open source software.[3][5]

- **Codification of the Open Source Definition (circa 1998)** — The Open Source Definition, derived from earlier free software principles, is formalized and maintained by OSI, specifying ten conditions that define open source software licensing.[3][5]

- **Growth of OSI-approved licenses (2000s–2010s)** — OSI’s license list becomes widely referenced, and its approval is treated as the standard for licenses that comply with the Open Source Definition across industry and government.[3][5]

- **Expansion into research and policy (by mid-2020s)** — OSI publishes analytical work such as the “State of Open Source” report, highlighting strategic drivers for open source adoption (geopolitics, security, compliance) and strengthens its policy voice around AI, security, and sustainability.[1][5]

- **Engagement with maintainer sustainability (2020s)** — OSI becomes an active participant in initiatives like “Maintainer Month,” explicitly recognizing maintainers as stewards of “critical digital infrastructure” and aligning its advocacy with their sustainability.[2][1]

## Impact

- **Impact on society**
  - OSI’s stewardship of the Open Source Definition and licensing norms underpins a vast amount of digital infrastructure, enabling governments, nonprofits, and companies to rely on open source software with clearer legal and policy guardrails.[5][3]
  - By recognizing and advocating for maintainers as guardians of “critical digital infrastructure,” OSI contributes to broader awareness of the human labor sustaining essential software used by societies worldwide.[2][1]

- **Impact on innovation**
  - OSI’s codification and maintenance of the Open Source Definition created a widely accepted baseline for what counts as open source, accelerating the spread of open collaboration models and license interoperability across the software industry.[5][3]
  - Its role in clarifying “open source” in the context of AI influences how AI models and tools are licensed and shared, shaping emerging norms around open source AI innovation.[5][1]

- **Impact on its industry or domain**
  - OSI functions as the de facto standards body for open source licensing; many organizations and commentators treat OSI approval as the authoritative signal that a license is genuinely open source, which strongly influences license design and adoption.[5][3]
  - Its State of Open Source reporting helps enterprise and policy stakeholders understand evolving risks and strategic considerations, influencing how organizations manage security, compliance, and operational practices in open source consumption and contribution.[1]

- **Historical significance**
  - Since the late 1990s, OSI has played a central role in the historical codification and mainstreaming of open source software, helping move the concept from a niche developer movement to a standard component of commercial and governmental IT strategies.[3][5]
