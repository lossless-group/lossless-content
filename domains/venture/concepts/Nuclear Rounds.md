> [!info] **Perplexity Query** (2025-08-16T15:47:38.694Z)
> **Question:**
> Explain why Venture Capital has started to include enormous rounds at enormous valuations.  Include the concept of "Kingmaking" and "Kingmakers"
> 
> ### **Response from sonar-pro**:

[[Vocabulary/Venture Capital]] has shifted toward **enormous rounds at enormous valuations** because a small number of companies—especially in fields like artificial intelligence—are attracting nearly half of all available funding, a phenomenon driven by structural changes, investor strategy, and market dynamics. [^k7ptbx] [^m9jnru] [^al3zcv] This consolidation is not just a transient trend but marks a new era where venture capital firms engage in “kingmaking,” betting on select firms they believe can dominate entire industries. [^k7ptbx]

### Why Are Rounds So Large?

- **Concentration of Capital:** In 2025, **41% of all U.S. venture capital dollars went to just 10 companies**, mostly in AI. [^k7ptbx] This is a dramatic increase from previous years when funding was more evenly distributed. The largest deals, like OpenAI’s historic $40 billion round, reflect investors funneling massive resources into a few perceived “winners”. [^k7ptbx] [^fytjk6] [^al3zcv]

- **Structural Factors:** Advances in technology have lowered the cost for some startups but in sectors like AI and life sciences, building cutting-edge products requires vast capital for data, compute, and talent. [^m9jnru] [^gq162f] This drives larger fundraising needs, especially as global players race to establish dominance.

- **Investor Dynamics:** Growth equity, crossover funds, and even hedge funds are chasing outsized returns in late-stage, pre-IPO companies, pushing round sizes up as they compete to get into the perceived leaders before public markets. [^m9jnru]

- **Market Uncertainty:** Economic volatility, high interest rates, and subdued IPO markets mean investors prefer “safer” bets – namely, established companies with clear market momentum and strong technical moats. [^gq162f]

### The Kingmaking Dynamic

- **Kingmaking Defined:** In venture capital, “kingmaking” refers to **VCs using their capital and network to help select startups become category leaders (“kings”)**. [^k7ptbx] Instead of spreading risk across many investments, VCs focus on a few and give them resources to outpace rivals.

- **Kingmakers:** These are the large funds, strategic investors, and tech conglomerates (e.g., SoftBank, Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet) that can pour billions into a single round. [^fytjk6] [^al3zcv] Their presence in a fundraising round acts as an endorsement, attracting further interest and validation.

- **Winner-Takes-All Markets:** Sectors like AI reward scale—more compute, more proprietary data, faster launches. The first to deploy significant capital accelerates its advantage, reinforcing the kingmaker logic. [^gq162f]

### Supporting and Contextual Trends

- **Mega-Rounds in Perspective:** The number of $100 million-plus rounds peaked in 2021 and dipped in 2022–2023, but rebounded in 2024–2025—driven especially by these kingmaking bets in AI, not by a broader market recovery. [^m9jnru] [^gq162f]

- **Global View:** The U.S. dominates these mega-rounds, with 64% of global VC funding. Other regions like Europe and China have cooled, but India is emerging with big bets in fintech and mobility. [^gq162f]

- **Seed Stage Swells:** Even early-stage startups are riding this trend, seeing deal sizes swell—again, often driven by kingmaker funds seeking to lock in future leaders early. [^gq162f]

### Summary of Main Drivers of Enormous VC Rounds

- **Scale rewards scale:** Sectors like AI demand huge upfront investments for world-class infrastructure and talent.
- **Investor risk aversion:** Money concentrates in perceived “safe bets” with momentum amid economic uncertainty.
- **Kingmaker mentality:** Leading investors actively pick and build future industry giants through capital and networks.
- **Network effects:** High-profile rounds attract further capital, reinforcing valuations and the dominance of “picked” companies.

This dynamic means venture capital now less often distributes risk across many startups and instead channels resources into both creating and anointing kings—making the role of kingmakers more pivotal than ever. [^k7ptbx] [^fytjk6] [^gq162f]
