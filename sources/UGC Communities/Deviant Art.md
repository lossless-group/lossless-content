*DeviantArt is a long‑running online art community and gallery platform where millions of creators share work, get feedback, and build niche fandoms and subcultures.*

Launched in **2000** as an online community for user‑generated artwork, DeviantArt has evolved into a large **digital art and social network platform** hosting drawings, paintings, photography, literature, 3D work, and more. It was founded by Scott Jarkoff, Angelo Sotira, and Matthew Stephens, and later acquired by design software company Wix in 2017. The site positions itself as a place “where art and community thrive,” letting users explore “over 650 million pieces of art” and connect with fellow artists and enthusiasts. Consultants return to it as a living laboratory of visual trends, fandoms, and creator‑economy dynamics that often surface years before they hit mainstream brands.

# Type and Format

- **Type:** This source is a **website** and **online community platform** for sharing and discovering artwork and related media.

- **Format details**
  - DeviantArt operates as a primarily **digital‑only** platform accessible via web and mobile apps, with no print edition.
  - Core features include user profiles, galleries, collections, groups, commissions, and a social feed for following artists and favoriting works.
  - It runs on a **freemium model**: basic membership is free, with paid Core Membership offering additional features such as increased customization, statistics, and higher visibility.
  - The platform was **founded in 2000** and has been owned by **Wix.com** since its acquisition in 2017.

# The People Behind It

- DeviantArt was **founded in 2000** by **Scott Jarkoff, Angelo Sotira, and Matthew Stephens** as an online community to share and discuss artwork and related creations.
- In 2017, **Wix.com Ltd.** acquired DeviantArt for about $36 million, integrating it into Wix’s broader web‑creation ecosystem while maintaining DeviantArt as a distinct brand and community.
- Post‑acquisition, DeviantArt’s leadership has included roles such as CEO and community leads within Wix’s organization, with a focus on evolving the platform’s design, product features (e.g., Eclipse redesign), and content policies.

# Catalog of Notable Works

Because DeviantArt is a **community platform** rather than a single authored work, its “catalog” consists of **features, sections, and community structures** rather than chapters or episodes.

- **User Galleries and Collections** — Each artist maintains a gallery of uploads (drawings, photos, digital art, literature) and can organize works into folders and curated collections, forming the basic unit of discovery.
- **Groups** — Community‑run hubs focused on specific genres (e.g., anime, furry art, fan‑art for particular IPs, photography categories) where members submit work to shared galleries and participate in challenges or contests.
- **Commissions & DeviantArt Protect** — Tools enabling artists to offer paid commissions and to get some protection and verification around their work, reflecting the platform’s role in the creator economy.
- **Eclipse Interface** — A major redesign of the site’s UI/UX introduced under Wix ownership, emphasizing a modern feed, portfolio‑style profiles, and a more social, personalized experience.
- **Mature and Fan‑Art Ecosystems** — Large bodies of fan‑art, OC (original character) communities, and mature / NSFW‑gated content that have shaped internet aesthetics and fandom norms.

(Individual artworks and artists number in the hundreds of millions, so this section focuses on **platform‑level features** rather than specific pieces.)

# Why It Matters to Innovators

- DeviantArt offers a **real‑time view of visual culture evolution**, letting innovators watch how styles, memes, and genres emerge, peak, and mutate long before they appear in mainstream advertising or product design.
- It is an early and enduring example of the **creator economy**, with commissions, fan‑funded work, and reputation‑based discovery illustrating models for monetizing creativity in digital communities.
- The platform highlights tensions around **IP, fan‑art, and platform policy**, giving innovators a sandbox for thinking about community norms, rights management, and co‑creation with passionate users.
- DeviantArt showcases how **niche communities self‑organize** via groups, tags, and subcultures, offering patterns relevant to building vertical networks, fandom‑centered products, or community‑led innovation.
- Its long history (since 2000) provides a longitudinal case study in **platform redesign and trust** (e.g., the Eclipse rollout, AI‑art debates, content policy changes) that map directly to issues like [[concepts/Platform Governance]], [[concepts/Community-led Innovation]], and [[concepts/Creator Economy|Creator Economy]].
