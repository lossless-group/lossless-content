### Main Content

Andrej Karpathy specializes in **deep learning**—a subset of machine learning focusing on artificial neural networks that imitate the human brain’s ability to learn from large datasets—and computer vision, which enables computers to interpret and understand images and videos. After earning a Bachelor’s degree in Computer Science and Physics from the University of Toronto and a Master’s from the University of British Columbia, Karpathy completed his PhD at [[organizations/Stanford University|Stanford University]] under Fei-Fei Li. His research particularly focused on the intersection of natural language processing and computer vision, exploring how machines can derive detailed descriptions from images (for example, identifying not just a cat in a photo, but describing its color, pose, and surroundings). [^b5luyt] [^913gnr] [^kgnf2p]

At Tesla, Karpathy led the AI team responsible for building and training neural networks powering **Autopilot**—Tesla’s driver-assistance and self-driving capabilities. These neural networks process vast amounts of real-world driving data to recognize objects, predict behavior, and make split-second decisions needed for safe vehicle autonomy. Under Karpathy’s guidance, Tesla developed advanced systems that allowed its cars to detect lane boundaries, respond to traffic lights, and avoid obstacles in complex urban environments. [^b5luyt] [^cm4i79]

Beyond his industry achievements, Karpathy's impact on **education and open-source AI tools** is profound. He was the primary instructor for Stanford's first deep learning course, CS231n: Convolutional [[Vocabulary/Neural Networks|Neural Networks]] for Visual Recognition, which grew rapidly to become one of the university’s largest classes. He also developed open-source libraries like ConvNetJS, making deep learning more accessible for students and practitioners worldwide. [^913gnr] [^kgnf2p] His straightforward teaching style and public engagement (through blogs, talks, and YouTube channels) have inspired a new generation of AI researchers.

Karpathy's contributions extend to OpenAI, where he worked on generative models and reinforcement learning, further enabling breakthroughs in AI systems that can learn from trial and error and generate imaginative outputs. Karpathy has been publicly recognized, being named an Innovator Under 35 by MIT Technology Review in 2020. [^913gnr]
