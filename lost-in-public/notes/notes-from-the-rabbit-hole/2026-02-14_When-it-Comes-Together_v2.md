# When It Comes Together

In late 2025, I was hired as a consultant for two emerging venture capital firms already in their fundraise, yet suddenly getting requests from more sophisticated capital providers. Among other work, both needed to backfill investment memorandums for their datarooms. One firm needed roughly a dozen memos on existing portfolio companies, and six on capital (LP) commitments they had made to individual investors managing their own micro-fund (Solo GPs). The other needed similar materials on twenty portfolio companies plus polished write-ups on eleven pipeline companies, presentable enough to share with prospective investors. In total, I faced over forty investment memorandums, many on companies outside my direct area of expertise.  Both firms were on fundraising timelines that left no room for delay.

Writing investment memos is work I know well. A strong memo in my style typically runs six to fifteen pages: and covers the firm methodology for opportunity analysis, including company overview, market context, competitive landscape, financial profile, and an investment thesis. Done manually with proper research, one memo takes a full working day by a mature and speedy professional, at a level unfortunately only forged under high-pressure work environments. Forty plus memos meant over eight weeks of heads-down writing — time neither I nor my clients had. I had to make a judgment call about how to deliver.

The conventional path was clear: grind through them one by one. It would be slow and exhausting, but predictable. With a decade as a Partner in VC, I knew I could produce quality work this way, uniquely good and at rapid speed.

The second option was to use off-the-shelf AI tools — paste in some notes, prompt a large language model, and clean up the output. I tested this approach and found the results too generic, the prompt engineering too redundant. The copy and paste and formatting became drudgery. The memos lacked the unique voice, structural rigor and source detail that sophisticated capital providers look for. They read like what they were: AI drafts with a human coat of paint.

A third option was to use some plugins I had built for myself over the year in my favorite Markdown notebook -- Obsidian.  At this point, my suite of various plugins could structure and fill out metadata, generate share images for social media, use research-based generative AI tools via API to generate custom and rigorous content, fetch open graph content for beautiful visualizations, and reformat citations while keeping a content wide reference library. These were tools that could help me develop investment memos with the cooperation of GenAI, but at greater speed and with less friction.  Yet, the workflow would still be one-by-one, with a lot of painstaking procedural work for each.

The final option was the riskiest but the one I was compelled to: build a custom agentic system that could orchestrate multiple AI models through a structured workflow — one agent to research, another to draft and edit, another to coordinate — producing memos that met a professional standard with citations, formatted footnotes, and integrated visuals from company materials. This is the path I chose, and it was a true judgment call rather than the result of analysis. I had no reliable basis for predicting whether it would work better than my other options. There was a real risk it would take longer to build than it would to personally write every memo.

Hesitation and risk aversion was well-founded. Throughout 2025, I had accumulated a trail of unfruitful attempts at building applications powered by AI. I had built prototype applications on no-code platforms like Bolt and Lovable, and watched them overwrite their own progress when managed poorly. Other fruitless endeavors in vibe coding stemmed from previous judgment calls that had yet to see real results, even personal ones. Early on the AI adoption curve, I observed that the native file formats of all AI models were open and simple standards: Markdown, Markup, JSON, and CSV. This is not apparent in chat apps or work studios, but becomes unmistakable when accessing models through their APIs. So a somewhat radical conviction took hold: I needed to convert all my project work into those basal file formats and work only in those formats. I would abandon the known and feature-rich world of Microsoft and Apple and forge into workflows totally new to me.

What followed were misadventures in vibe coding and content management. I developed automation scripts to maintain data quality across files, only to introduce new errors worse than the ones I was fixing. I built a system to watch my file directories and generate metadata templates automatically, only to spend days debugging cascading failures that silently corrupted files before I noticed. Each attempt had a sound rationale but produced negative progress. If measured by the result in the moment — better file management — it was sunk cost. The pattern was discouraging enough that any reasonable discussion of my track record would have a strong set of arguments against trying again.

But a different pattern was also forming beneath: a mental model and workflow for how to best cooperate with AI. Each dead end taught me something concrete about where AI tools break down and where they work reliably. I learned that AI is most effective when constrained to specific, well-defined tasks rather than given broad, ambiguous instructions. I learned that managing instruction files that anticipate the needs of the AI models is more important than the code or content itself. I learned that breaking complex workflows into small, isolated steps — each with clear inputs and outputs — produces far more reliable results than asking a single tool to do everything. I developed disciplined habits using version control with Git and GitHub, as evident in my  3,021 commits in 2025, compared to 258 in 2024. And I had converted my working files into open, plain-text formats that AI models process natively, using code to send them to APIs and parsing the returned files with seamless integration.  This meant I had a clean foundation to build on. None of these lessons came from analysis or planning, they would be clunky inputs to any decision making system. They had formed into insights forged from stubborn repetition, paying close attention with constant reflection, and iteration towards a self-created process that I felt comfortable with, even proud of.

This accumulated process knowledge built intuition is what made the judgment call one I was eager to make. I would use my newfound vibe coding skills to build an agentic investment memo orchestrator: agents accessing different, best-fit AI models providing coherent, single objective agents to research, draft, edit, prevent hallucination, with a coordinator agent managing the workflow between them. 

I chose a framework designed for exactly this, LangGraph, and AI-assisted coding to build it. The specification and architecture took a day. Within a few days, the system could produce a draft six-to-fifteen-page investment memo in under ten minutes close to the output of a professional.  By the end of the second week, draft memos were complete with sourced citations, formatted footnotes, and key visuals pulled from company slide decks, and accurately told the story evident in the confidential deck and materials provided by the company. After another week of refinement, I added the ability to ingest an analyze financials and forecasts, follow a proprietary outline defined at the venture capital firm level, unique to them and in their voice, and export the final output as a branded PDF using custom templates defined in HTML and CSS, a bonus that it could be rendered on their website.

The work that had taken me a full day now took fifteen minutes. If the client didn't like the draft, I could create another draft according to their preferred analysis or messaging in under fifteen minutes. I delivered all forty-plus memos within the timeframe my clients needed, at a quality level that both impressed my clients and held up to investor scrutiny from serious capital providers. The two firms were able to proceed with their fundraising processes within the timeframe they hoped. Given I have no intent on building a SaaS business for this, the project is an open repository on GitHub and I have sent it to various venture capital groups.  Six different firms have sent me deals they were looking at, and I turned around impressive drafts in less than the time it takes to go get a coffee. 

So, a bold judgement call was based on pattern recognition built from a year of attempts with nothing to show, yet delivered for my clients and gave me the moment where it all came together and AI gave me the superpowers, in this case, the hype had promised. I had no spreadsheet or model that said "build this system and it will work." I had an accumulation of hard-won knowledge, a body of prior work that created the right preconditions. I had accumulated understanding that led to my judgement that I could pull it off, and it would be better than alternatives that felt too slow, too painstaking, or too shallow. The attempts could finally come together and create real results.
