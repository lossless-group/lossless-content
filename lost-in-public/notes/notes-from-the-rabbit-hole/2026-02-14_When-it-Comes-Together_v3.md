# When It Comes Together

In late 2025, I was hired as a consultant for two emerging venture capital firms in their fundraise, yet suddenly fielding requests from more discerning institutional investors. Both needed to backfill investment memorandums for their datarooms. One firm needed eighteen memos across portfolio companies and capital commitments to Solo GPs. The other needed materials on twenty portfolio companies plus polished write-ups on twelve pipeline companies fit to share as a carrot. In total, I faced over forty investment memorandums, many on companies outside my area of expertise, on fundraising timelines that left no room for delay.

Writing investment memos is work I know well. A strong memo in my style would be six to fifteen pages covering company overview, market context, competitive landscape, financial profile, and an investment thesis. Done manually with proper research, a memo takes a full working day by any mature professional, and that speed is limited to those forged under high-pressure work environments. Forty-plus memos meant over eight weeks of heads-down writing — time neither I nor my clients had. I had to make a judgment call about how to deliver.

The conventional path was clear: grind through them one by one. Slow and exhausting, but predictable. With a decade as a Partner in VC, I produce quality work this way at rapid speed.

The second option was to use off-the-shelf AI apps and tools — paste in some notes, keep a RAG library, prompt a large language model, and clean up the output. I tested this approach and found the results too generic, the prompt engineering too redundant, the copy-paste-and-format cycle its own form of drudgery. The memos lacked the unique voice, structural rigor, and source detail that sophisticated capital providers look for. They read like what they were: AI drafts with a human coat of paint.

A third option was to use plugins I had built over the year in Obsidian, my preferred Markdown notebook. My suite of plugins could structure metadata, generate content using research-based generative AI via API, fetch open graph images for rich visualizations, and maintain a content-wide citation and reference library. My plugins could help me develop investment memos at greater speed and less friction, but the work would still be one-by-one with painstaking procedural work.

The final option was the riskiest and the one I was compelled to choose: build a custom agentic system that could orchestrate multiple AI models through a structured workflow, each model assigned to the task it does best. I had no reliable basis for predicting whether it would work, and a real risk it would take longer to build than to personally write every memo.

Hesitation and risk aversion was well-founded. Throughout 2025, I had accumulated a trail of unfruitful attempts at building applications powered by AI. I had built prototype applications on no-code platforms like Bolt and Lovable, and watched them overwrite their own progress when managed poorly. Other fruitless endeavors in vibe coding stemmed from previous judgment calls that had yet to see real results, even personal ones. Early on the AI adoption curve, I observed that the native file formats of all AI models were open and simple standards: Markdown, Markup, JSON, and CSV. This is not apparent in chat apps or work studios, but becomes unmistakable when accessing models through their APIs. So a somewhat radical conviction took hold: I needed to convert all my project work into those basal file formats and work only in those formats. I would abandon the known and feature-rich world of Microsoft and Apple and forge into workflows totally new to me.

What followed were misadventures in vibe coding and content management. I developed automation scripts to maintain data quality across files, only to introduce new errors worse than the ones I was fixing. I built a system to watch my file directories and generate metadata templates automatically, only to spend days debugging cascading failures that silently corrupted files before I noticed. Each attempt had a sound rationale but produced negative progress. If measured by the result in the moment — better file management — it was sunk cost. The pattern was discouraging enough that any reasonable discussion of my track record would have a strong set of arguments against trying again.

But a different pattern was forming beneath: a mental model for how to cooperate with AI effectively. Each dead end taught me something concrete. AI is most effective when constrained to specific, well-defined tasks rather than given broad instructions. Managing instruction files that anticipate the needs of AI models is more important than the code or content itself. Breaking complex workflows into small, isolated steps — each with clear inputs and outputs — produces far more reliable results than asking a single tool to do everything. I developed disciplined version control habits, as evident in my 3,021 Git commits in 2025 compared to 258 in 2024. And the months spent converting everything into basal file formats meant I had a clean, AI-native foundation to build on. None of these lessons came from analysis or planning; they would be clunky inputs to any decision-making system. They were insights forged from stubborn repetition, careful observation, and iteration toward a self-created process I felt comfortable with, even proud of.

This accumulated intuition is what made the decision feel bold yet natural. I would build an agentic investment memo orchestrator using LangGraph, a framework designed for multi-agent coordination. The architecture assigned each agent a different, best-fit AI model for a single clear objective: Perplexity for deep research with sourced citations, GPT for drafting and editing prose, Claude as the orchestrator coordinating the workflow and preventing hallucination. I used AI-assisted coding tools — primarily Windsurf and Claude Code — to build it. The specification and architecture took a day. Within a few days, the system could ingest a company's slide deck and confidential materials, run the research agent, pass findings to the drafting agent, and produce a six-to-fifteen-page investment memo in under ten minutes that read like the work of a seasoned professional. By the end of the second week, draft memos included sourced citations, formatted footnotes in extended Markdown, and key visuals from company decks. After another week, I added the ability to ingest and analyze financials, follow a proprietary outline unique to each firm in their voice, and export as a branded PDF using HTML and CSS templates — with the bonus that it could render on their website.

The work that had taken me a full day now took fifteen minutes. If the client did not like the draft, I could produce another according to their preferred analysis or messaging just as quickly. I delivered all forty-plus memos within the timeframe my clients needed, at a quality level that impressed them and held up to investor scrutiny. Both firms proceeded with their fundraising on schedule. The project is now an open repository on GitHub. Six different venture capital firms have since sent me deals to evaluate, and I turned around impressive drafts in less time than it takes to go get a coffee.

This judgment was not informed by historical data or certainty from analysis, it was a combined vision and optimism resting on a year of experimentation.  Looking at the same scenario, many would not conclude "build this system and it will work." I had an accumulation of hard-won knowledge, the right preconditions, and a willingness to bet on my vision when alternatives felt unideal.  I had been paying close attention, gone deep in the rabbit holes, and could recognize a moment that could bring in pieces were finally ready to come together.
